Get to know how the asymmetric digital subscriber line (ADSL) transmission technology works, followed by a brief overview of xDSL technologies

Everybody who owns a PC and has Internet connection wants to access the Internet at high speeds and have large bandwidth. Services like voice-over-IP and video-streaming require high-speed Internet access for effective operation. Unshielded copper pairs used in telephone network carry voice signals in the frequency band of 300 to 3400 Hz but are capable of transporting information at much higher rates. These cables have been used to transport data in LANs up to 10 MHz or more. Line attenuation in these lines up to about 6 MHz is of the order of 0.7 dB/kHz with almost constant group delay, producing very little pulse distortion and therefore very few bit errors.

Digital subscriber line (DSL) is a transmission technology that utilizes this available bandwidth to provide high-speed access to the Internet. xDSL transmission technologies use digital multi-tone (DMT) modulation to carry data across the copper pair. Individual xDSL technologies vary from each other in the amount of bandwidth allocated for upstream and downstream data.

Asymmetric digital subscriber line (ADSL). This DSL-based technology enables transmission and reception of data at speeds higher than legacy copper media. The modulation technique used allows several bits to be represented by one transmission symbol. In ADSL, bitrate allocation for a channel within the available bandwidth is not the same for other channels and hence the term ‘asymmetric’. In other words, the upstream bandwidth is smaller than the downstream bandwidth.

ADSL caters specifically to connections between ISPs and customers. The Internet is used largely for downloading files, HTML, and graphical content. Processes like uploading files or other content to servers are limited to very few users. Hence the bandwidth required for downstream data (from ISP to client) is more than that required for upstream data.

Another advantage of using ADSL is that it is possible to send a legacy telephone signal. This means that an ADSL-based communication system can be integrated into the existing telephone network. A device (typically, a splitter) at the customer-end separates the phone signals from the ADSL signals. Deploying ADSL in a telephone network to provide high-speed data services is a very attractive proposition to a service provider.ADSL offers an upstream data rate of 500 kbps and a downstream data rate of up to 8 Mbps. ADSL Lite, another variant of the ADSL standard, offers upstream speeds of up to 500 kbps and downstream speeds of 1.5 Mbps

A typical ADSL set-up

The two main elements of an ADSL set-up are the digital subscriber-line access multiplexed (DSLAM) placed in the neighborhood or central office, and the ADSL modem placed in the customer’s premises. The ADSL modem performs the task of converting the ADSL physical layer signal into a format\interface that can be understood by a computer or any other equipment and connected to it.The DSLAM behaves like a concentrator. It provides multiple DSL connections for access to the bandwidth available to the DSLAM from the backbone. These multiple DSL connections can then be connected via copper to the DSL modems in the customer’s premises.There are basically two types of DSLAMs, namely, the CO DSLAM and the Remote DSLAM. The CO DSLAM is a large-capacity concentrator that is generally placed in the service provider’s central office. The Remote DSLAM sits in the digital loop carrier (DLC) system in the neighborhood or basement of the building. In a typical ADSL set-up. The POTS splitter at the central office filters out the incoming ADSL signal and diverts the voice traffic to the voice switch. The data traffic is directed towards the CO DSLAM. The CO DSLAM is connected to the WAN backbone and the voice switch is connected to the PSTN network.

The pair from the DLC DSLAM is terminated into the DSL\POTS splitter in the customer’s premises. The splitter, which is basically a low-pass amplifier, removes the POTS signal from the incoming ADSL signal. The filtered ADSL signal is then given to an ADSL modem or router that provides an Ethernet or serial connection to the host computer or other equipment. ADSL Lite. Also known as Splitterless, Universal ADSL, G.992,2, or G.Lite, ADSL Lite is a lower-speed version of ADSL that dispenses with the need for the phone company to provide or maintain an end-user POTS splitter. Reducing the data rate causes less interference in the POTS signal, and therefore a POTS splitter is not required. This result in a significant cost saving that can be passed onto the customers. Customers will be able to connect G.Lite modems directly to the phone jacks. This plug-‘n’-play functionality makes ADSL Lite more attractive to the mass consumer market. Promoted by Universal ADSL Working Group (UAWG), ADSL Lite provides downstream speeds of 1.5 Mbps and upstream speeds of 500 kbps.

How does ADSL work?


Any kind of signal (a voltage or a current waveform) can be broken up into its individual frequency components, according to Fourier theory. The interval between the frequency components may be theoretically infinitesimally small. Following the same principle, it is possible to obtain spectral components of a signal using a filter.The output of the filter will be a signal of a particular frequency called the center frequency of the filter. Using multiple filters, having the requisite centre frequencies, can separate the individual frequency components of a signal. Mathematically, the Fourier series or transform for that signal describes this process. For signals in the digital domain (analogue signals converted to digital), the most frequently used algorithm to obtain the spectral content is known as the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). Inverse FFT (IFFT) describes a process to convert a frequency domain signal to its time domain equivalent.

To utilize the 6MHz bandwidth available on an unshielded copper pair to provide high-speed data services, ADSL uses the DMT technique. The fundamental carrier is broken into multiple carriers of smaller bandwidths. Each carrier is then quadrature amplitude modulated (QAM) to carry 2 bits per second to 15 bits per second.

The American National Standard Institute (ANSI) Standard T1.413 (1997) provides a frequency range of 0 to 26 kHz for the POTS signal. The 26-1130kHz frequency band accommodates 256 sub-channels, each 4.3125kHz wide. The centers of these sub-channels are also kept separated by 4.3125 kHz. This method is referred to as frequency division multiplexing (FDM). The individual carriers in the upstream and downstream ranges are quadrature-amplitude modulated to carry 2 bits per second per hertz to 15 bits per second per hertz. The bit-carrying rate of each band is allocated adaptively during the initialization process, i.e. the bit-carrying rate is decided by the signal-to-noise (S/N) ratio in the relevant channel. The larger the S/N ratio, the larger the signal space allocated to the band. If the S/N ratio of a band is too small, that bands may not be utilized.From Shannon’s theorem, the maximum information that can be transmitted per channel is 64.7 kbps using QAM. Theoretically, this gives a maximum transfer rate of above 16 Mbps for 256 channels. However, due to attenuation at high frequencies and limitations of the circuit design, only about half this capacity is used.

Another method of allocating channels for upstream and downstream bands in ADSL is based on echo-compensation. In echo-compensation, the frequencies allocated for upstream data flow are used for both upstream and downstream data flow. This leads to a higher capacity for downstream flow since the lower 112 kHz of the ADSL range contains better channels; attenuation rises at higher frequencies. This ADSL mode is defined in Category-2 ADSL.Echo-compensation technique is used to send and receive information on the same frequency channel at the same time. The transceiver retains the last transmitted data. The data received by the transceiver contains both the received data as well as the transmitted data (which is actually an echo). The transceiver then uses the retained transmitted information to cancel out the echo from the received signal. By using highly precise echo-cancellation algorithms and high-speed DSPs, it is possible to segregate the transmitted and received information to a high degree of accuracy.

The ADSL Lite standard does away with the need for a POTS splitter at the customer end by reducing the number of channels from 256 to 128. The upstream data rate is still kept at around 512 kbps, but the downstream data rate is reduced to around 1.5 Mbps. By reducing the data rate, the interference caused by the emphasized high frequencies on POTS frequencies is reduced

The working of an ADSL modem

Data to be transmitted (upstream data at the customer’s end and downstream data at the exchange end) is first applied to an encoder that allocates it to ‘N’ channels of the DMT signal. This is done in accordance with the bit-loading table that is set up during the link set-up process. This table contains the information required to determine the number of bits per second that each channel can handle.

The encoder also provides forward error correction (FEC) with a Reed Solomon code. In FEC technique, the error correction information is stored within the transmitted data itself. Therefore it is not necessary for data to be retransmitted in the case of an error. The parallel digital stream is then applied to an IFFT processor that converts the ‘N’-bit wide frequency domain samples to 2N time-domain samples. The parallel bits are transcoded into a serial format whereupon a cyclic prefix is added. The echo-canceller creates an image of the sent and received signals that blots out the real echoes when they come along. It is set up during the link set-up process. The output of the echo-canceller is applied to a digital-to-analogue converter and then diplexer onto the telephone line. The incoming signal is applied to the echo-canceller via the analogue-to-digital converter.

The echo-canceller removes the transmitted image from the received signals, thus separating the upstream and the downstream data. The downstream data is then applied to an equalizer that adjusts for the higher power levels of the higher frequencies in the received signals. The equalizer is set up during the link set-up process for requisite equalization constants.

After the cyclic prefix has been removed and the signal has been transcoded into a parallel format, it is applied to a FFT processor that converts the ‘N’-bit wide time samples to equivalent frequency domain signals. The decoder then reconstructs the bits contained in the DMT channels into the correct sequence by using the bit-loading table it is programmed with. The decoder also provides FEC with Reed Solomon code to ensure that any bit errors are corrected.

The Suitor

Other xDSL technologies Based on the DMT technique, various other DSL technologies like R-ADSL, HDSL, SDSL, and VDSL have been developed. These technologies differ in terms of signal distance, speed, and the symmetry of upstream and downstream traffic. The xDSL technology is used only in the local loop in an end-to-end remote architecture.Rate-adaptive digital subscriber line (R-ADSL). R-ADSL operates with the same transmission rates as ADSL, but adjusts dynamically to varying lengths and qualities of the twisted-pair local lines. It makes possible to connect over different lines at varying speeds. Connection speed is negotiated by the end-points when the line synchronizes up or as a result of a signal from the central office.

High-bitrate digital subscriber line (HDSL). HDSL is a symmetric technology that provides the same amount of bandwidth for upstream and downstream traffic. It offers speeds of 2.048 Mbps over two copper pairs with operating distances of 3.6 kilometers to 4.6 kilometers, and is ideal for connecting PBX systems, digital local loops, points of presence, Internet servers, and campus-based networks. HDSL II, another technology proposed within ANSI and ETSI, offers the same performance of HDSL, but over a single pair.

Single-line digital subscriber line (SDSL). SDSL also offers symmetrical T1\E1 speeds but operates over a single copper pair up to a distance of 3 kilometers. It is a precursor to HDSL II. Very high-bitrate digital subscriber line (VDSL). VDSL is the fastest xDSL technology, supporting downstream rates of 13 to 52 Mbps and upstream rates of 1.5 to 2.3 Mbps over a single copper-pair wire. It provides a cost-effective alternative to fiber-to-the-home. This asymmetric technology operates over 305 meters to 1.4 kilometers only from the central office. One method of VDSL deployment is to run a fiber up to an optical network unit (ONU) in the neighborhood and then run copper pair to the customer’s premises within 1.2 kilometers of the ONU. The high bitrate support makes VDSL ideal for services like HDTV.
